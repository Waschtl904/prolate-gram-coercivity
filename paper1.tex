\begin{abstract}
Let $\PW$ be the Paley--Wiener space of $\omega$-bandlimited functions
and $V_{N,c}:=\operatorname{span}\{\psi_0^{(c)},\dots,\psi_{N-1}^{(c)}\}$
the $N$-dimensional prolate subspace at time-bandwidth product $c=\omega T$.
For $N$ distinct sampling points $\mathbf{p}\subset[-T,T]$ we study
the prolate Gram form $\QM(f)=\sum_{j=1}^N|f(p_j)|^2$ on $V_{N,c}$.

The nontrivial content of this paper is two-fold.
First, we prove unisolvence for two concrete families of sampling sets:
random configurations and Gauss--PSWF quadrature nodes
(Propositions~\ref{prop:random}--\ref{prop:gauss}).
For uniform grids we explain the associated zero-counting problem,
but we do not claim a theorem because $V_{N,c}$ is not known to be
a Chebyshev system in general.
Second, under an abstract sampling-quality condition~(T.2),
we prove a sharp quantitative comparison between discrete sampling and the prolate operator,
namely
$\QM(f)=\ip{\Kop f}{f}_{L^2}+R_N(f)$ with $|R_N(f)|\le\varepsilon_N\norm{f}^2$,
where the remainder measures how well discrete sampling approximates
the continuous quadratic form of~$\Kop$.
For Gauss--PSWF nodes we also record an explicit structural identity for the error matrix,
showing that the deviation from the reference operator is governed by the deviation of the
quadrature weights from uniformity. This yields a complete verification of~(T.2)
for that family once a quantitative weight-flatness estimate is available.

No claim is made about zeros of $\zeta(s)$, prime sampling, or limits
in which both $N$ and $c$ vary.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The Weil positivity criterion \cite{Weil1952,Bombieri2000,CC2019}
expresses the Riemann Hypothesis as positivity of an explicit
quadratic form over primes.
The prolate program of Connes--Consani--Moscovici
\cite{CCM_PNAS2022,CCM2025} seeks a spectral realization of this form
using the prolate concentration operator $\Kop$ on Paley--Wiener space.

The present paper isolates and proves a finite-dimensional coercivity
statement motivated by that broader program.
This paper establishes a finite-dimensional coercivity principle that is
independent of any number-theoretic input.
The linear-algebraic core is elementary: coercivity is equivalent to
invertibility of the evaluation matrix $M(\mathbf{p},c)$.
The real content lies in two issues:
(i) identifying concrete families of sampling sets for which
unisolvence can actually be proved, and
(ii) quantifying the gap between the discrete sampling form and the
continuous quadratic form of $\Kop$.

Layer~A proves unisolvence for random sampling and Gauss--PSWF nodes,
and records the uniform-grid case as an open zero-counting problem.
Layer~B proves the spectral decomposition under condition~(T.2).
Layer~C contains heuristic discussion of prime sampling.

No statement is made about zeros of $\zeta(s)$.
The limit $N\to\infty$ and coupled limits in which $N$ and $c$ vary
are outside the scope of this paper.

\section{Setup and notation}\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Basic objects}

We use the engineering Fourier convention
$\widehat{f}(\xi)=\int_{\mathbb{R}}f(x)e^{-2\pi ix\xi}\,dx$.

\begin{remark}[Fourier convention and bandwidth]
Under this convention the Paley--Wiener space of bandwidth $\omega$
corresponds to Fourier support in
$[-\omega/(2\pi),\omega/(2\pi)]$.
Many references use the convention
$\widehat f(\xi)=\int f(x)e^{-i\xi x}\,dx$,
for which the same space corresponds to $[-\omega,\omega]$.
\end{remark}

The \emph{Paley--Wiener space} $\PW$ consists of all
$f\in L^2(\mathbb{R})$ with Fourier support in
$[-\omega/(2\pi),\omega/(2\pi)]$.
It is a reproducing-kernel Hilbert space with kernel
$K_{\PW}(x,y)=\tfrac{\omega}{\pi}\operatorname{sinc}(\omega(x-y)/\pi)$
and $K_{\PW}(x,x)=\omega/\pi$ \cite[Eq.~(3)]{BonamiKaroui}.
Every $f\in\PW$ extends to an entire function of exponential type,
by the Paley--Wiener theorem.

The \emph{prolate concentration operator} is
\[
  \Kop f(x)=\int_{-T}^{T}\frac{\sin\omega(x-y)}{\pi(x-y)}f(y)\,dy,
  \qquad \Kop:\PW\to\PW.
\]
It is self-adjoint and compact on $\PW$, with simple eigenvalues
$1>\lambda_0(c)>\lambda_1(c)>\cdots>0$ and eigenfunctions
(PSWFs) $\{\psin\}_{n\ge 0}$ forming an orthonormal basis of $\PW$
\cite{Slepian1978,BonamiKaroui}.
Throughout, $c=\omega T$ and
\[
  V_{N,c}:=\operatorname{span}\{\psi_0^{(c)},\dots,\psi_{N-1}^{(c)}\}.
\]
This is an $N$-dimensional subspace of $\PW$ for each fixed pair $(N,c)$.
There is no canonical identification of $V_{N,c}$ across different values
of $c$.

\subsection{Gram matrix and reference matrix}

\begin{definition}[Prolate Gram matrix and form]\label{def:GramForm}
For distinct $\mathbf{p}=(p_1,\dots,p_N)\subset[-T,T]$, the
\emph{evaluation matrix} is $M(\mathbf{p},c)_{jn}:=\psi_n^{(c)}(p_j)$,
and the \emph{prolate Gram matrix} is
$\GVM:=M(\mathbf{p},c)^*M(\mathbf{p},c)$.

If $f=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}a_n\psin\in V_{N,c}$, then
$\norm{f}_{L^2}^2=\norm{\mathbf{a}}_2^2$ and
$\QM(f):=\sum_{j=1}^N|f(p_j)|^2=\ip{\GVM\mathbf{a}}{\mathbf{a}}$.
Write $\AM:=\sqrt{\lambda_{\min}(\GVM)}$ and
$\BM:=\sqrt{\lambda_{\max}(\GVM)}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Reference matrix and condition~(T.2)]\label{def:T2}
Define the diagonal matrix
\[
  G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}:=\operatorname{diag}(\lambda_0(c),\dots,\lambda_{N-1}(c)).
\]
This is the matrix of $\Kop|_{V_{N,c}}$ in the PSWF eigenbasis:
for $f=\sum_n a_n\psin$,
\[
  \ip{\Kop f}{f}_{L^2}
  =\sum_n\lambda_n(c)|a_n|^2
  =\ip{G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}\mathbf{a}}{\mathbf{a}}.
\]
Set $\EN:=\GVM-G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}$.
We say that $\mathbf{p}$ satisfies~(T.2) if
\[
  \norm{\EN}_2\le\varepsilon_N
  \qquad\text{and}\qquad
  \varepsilon_N<\lambda_{N-1}(c).
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Discrete versus continuous forms]
The matrix $\GVM$ arises from discrete point evaluation, whereas
$G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}$ represents the restriction of the continuous
operator $\Kop$ to $V_{N,c}$ in the PSWF basis.
Thus $\EN$ is a matrix-valued quadrature error.
Equivalently, condition~(T.2) may be interpreted as a compatibility condition
between discrete sampling and the spectral decomposition of the prolate operator.
\end{remark}

\section{Layer~A: Unisolvence and operator core}\label{sec:layerA}

\begin{definition}[Unisolvent set]\label{def:unisolvent}
A set $\mathbf{p}=(p_1,\dots,p_N)\subset[-T,T]$ of $N$ distinct points
is \emph{unisolvent for $V_{N,c}$} (equivalently, uniqueness of interpolation in $V_{N,c}$)
if the only $f\in V_{N,c}$ satisfying $f(p_j)=0$ for all $j$ is $f\equiv 0$.
Since $\dim V_{N,c}=N$, this is equivalent to bijectivity of the evaluation map
$\mathcal{E}_{\mathbf{p}}:V_{N,c}\to\mathbb{C}^N$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Why unisolvence is non-trivial for $V_{N,c}$]
For a Chebyshev system, every $N$-tuple of distinct points is unisolvent
\cite[Ch.~1]{KarlinStudden1966}.
The space $V_{N,c}$ is not known to be a Chebyshev system in general.
Hence unisolvence must be proved separately for specific families.
In particular, the natural zero-counting route for uniform grids remains open.
\end{remark}

\begin{lemma}[Identity principle for Paley--Wiener functions]\label{lem:identity}
If $f\in\PW$ vanishes on a nonempty interval, then $f\equiv 0$ on
$\mathbb R$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Every $f\in\PW$ extends to an entire function of exponential type.
If it vanishes on an interval, it has an accumulation set of zeros.
By the identity theorem for holomorphic functions, the entire extension
must vanish identically, hence $f\equiv 0$ on $\mathbb R$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Random sampling is almost surely unisolvent]
\label{prop:random}
Let $p_1,\dots,p_N$ be i.i.d. with a distribution absolutely continuous
with respect to Lebesgue measure on $[-T,T]$.
Then $\mathbf{p}$ is unisolvent for $V_{N,c}$ almost surely.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Non-unisolvence means $\det M(\mathbf{p},c)=0$.
Since each PSWF $\psi_n^{(c)}$ is a real-analytic function, the map
$(p_1,\dots,p_N)\mapsto\det M(\mathbf{p},c)$ is real analytic.
It is not identically zero since there exists at least one configuration
(for instance, Gauss--PSWF nodes from Proposition~\ref{prop:gauss})
for which the matrix has full rank.
The zero set of a nonzero real-analytic function has Lebesgue measure zero,
so absolute continuity implies that non-unisolvence occurs with probability zero.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Gauss--PSWF nodes are unisolvent]\label{prop:gauss}
Let $p_1<\cdots<p_N$ be Gauss--PSWF quadrature nodes with positive weights
$w_1,\dots,w_N$, arising from the Gaussian quadrature adapted to the PSWF basis,
and chosen so that the quadrature rule is exact on products
$f\overline{g}$ with $f,g\in V_{N,c}$; see \cite{Xiao2001}.
Then $\mathbf{p}$ is unisolvent for $V_{N,c}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Assume $f\in V_{N,c}$ and $f(p_j)=0$ for all $j$.
Since $|f|^2=f\overline{f}$ belongs to the exactness class,
\[
  \int_{-T}^{T}|f(x)|^2\,dx
  =\sum_{j=1}^N w_j |f(p_j)|^2
  =0.
\]
Hence $f=0$ almost everywhere on $[-T,T]$, thus everywhere on that interval
by continuity, and therefore $f\equiv 0$ on $\mathbb R$
by Lemma~\ref{lem:identity}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Explicit error representation for Gauss--PSWF nodes]
\label{prop:gauss_error}
Let $p_1<\cdots<p_N$ be Gauss--PSWF quadrature nodes with positive weights
$w_1,\dots,w_N$, and let $D:=\operatorname{diag}(w_1,\dots,w_N)$.
Assume that the quadrature rule is exact on products $f\overline g$ with
$f,g\in V_{N,c}$.
Then, in the PSWF basis,
\[
  M(\mathbf p,c)^* D\, M(\mathbf p,c)=G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}.
\]
Consequently,
\[
  \EN
  =\GVM-G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}
  =M(\mathbf p,c)^*(I-D)M(\mathbf p,c).
\]
In particular,
\[
  \norm{\EN}_2\le \norm{I-D}_2\,\norm{M(\mathbf p,c)}_2^2.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For basis vectors $\psi_m^{(c)},\psi_n^{(c)}\in V_{N,c}$, quadrature exactness gives
\[
  \sum_{j=1}^N w_j\,\psi_m^{(c)}(p_j)\overline{\psi_n^{(c)}(p_j)}
  =\int_{-T}^{T}\psi_m^{(c)}(x)\overline{\psi_n^{(c)}(x)}\,dx.
\]
Since the PSWFs diagonalize $\Kop$, the right-hand side equals
$\lambda_n(c)\,\delta_{mn}$.
Hence the matrix of the weighted discrete form in the PSWF basis is exactly
$G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}$, i.e.
\[
  M(\mathbf p,c)^* D\,M(\mathbf p,c)=G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}.
\]
Subtracting this identity from $\GVM=M(\mathbf p,c)^*M(\mathbf p,c)$ yields
\[
  \EN=M(\mathbf p,c)^*(I-D)M(\mathbf p,c).
\]
Finally, the operator-norm bound follows from submultiplicativity.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Interpretation of Proposition~\ref{prop:gauss_error}]
For Gauss--PSWF nodes, the deviation from the reference operator is entirely governed by
the deviation of the quadrature weights from uniformity.
Thus the comparison problem is not merely a generic quadrature issue; it can be read as a
compatibility question between discrete sampling and the spectral decomposition of the prolate operator.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Reduction of \textup{(T.2)} to weight flatness for Gauss--PSWF nodes]
\label{cor:gauss_weight_flatness}
In the setting of Proposition~\ref{prop:gauss_error}, assume in addition that
\[
  \delta_N:=\norm{I-D}_2<\frac{\lambda_{N-1}(c)}{N\omega/\pi}.
\]
Then condition~\textup{(T.2)} holds with
\[
  \varepsilon_N:=\delta_N\,\frac{N\omega}{\pi}.
\]
In particular, if $\delta_N\to0$ in a regime where $N\omega/\pi$ remains bounded,
then $\varepsilon_N\to0$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
By Proposition~\ref{prop:gauss_error},
\[
  \norm{\EN}_2\le \norm{I-D}_2\,\norm{M(\mathbf p,c)}_2^2.
\]
Since $\GVM=M(\mathbf p,c)^*M(\mathbf p,c)$, one has
$\norm{M(\mathbf p,c)}_2^2=\norm{\GVM}_2=\lambda_{\max}(\GVM)$.
By Theorem~\ref{thm:layerA}(II),
$\lambda_{\max}(\GVM)\le N\omega/\pi$.
Therefore
\[
  \norm{\EN}_2\le \delta_N\,\frac{N\omega}{\pi}=\varepsilon_N.
\]
The displayed hypothesis gives
$\varepsilon_N<\lambda_{N-1}(c)$, so Definition~\ref{def:T2} applies.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[What Corollary~\ref{cor:gauss_weight_flatness} does and does not prove]
The corollary reduces the verification of~\textup{(T.2)} for Gauss--PSWF nodes to a
pure weight-flatness estimate.
It does not by itself prove that $\delta_N\to0$; that is an additional quantitative
quadrature question.
Accordingly, the main unresolved issue for this family is no longer the matrix structure
of the error term, but the asymptotic behavior of the quadrature weights.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Uniform grids]
We do not claim unisolvence for uniform grids.
A natural approach would be to prove that every nonzero element of
$V_{N,c}$ has at most $N-1$ zeros in $(-T,T)$, but such a bound would
follow from a Chebyshev-type property that is not available in general
for PSWF spans. Establishing or disproving uniform-grid unisolvence is
therefore left open.
\end{remark}

\begin{lemma}[Pointwise bound via reproducing kernel]\label{lem:upper}
For every $f\in\PW$ and every $x\in\mathbb R$,
\[
  |f(x)|^2
  =|\ip{f}{K_x}_{L^2}|^2
  \le \norm{f}_{L^2}^2\,\norm{K_x}_{L^2}^2
  = K_{\PW}(x,x)\,\norm{f}_{L^2}^2
  = \frac{\omega}{\pi}\,\norm{f}_{L^2}^2.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{theorem}[Operator core]\label{thm:layerA}
Let $(N,c)$ be fixed and let $\mathbf{p}$ be unisolvent for $V_{N,c}$.
Then:
\begin{enumerate}[(I)]
\item $\QM(f)\ge \AM^2\norm{f}_{L^2}^2$ for all $f\in V_{N,c}$.
\item $\QM(f)\le N(\omega/\pi)\norm{f}_{L^2}^2$ for all $f\in V_{N,c}$.
\end{enumerate}
In particular, these bounds hold unconditionally for random sampling
almost surely and for Gauss--PSWF nodes.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Unisolvence is equivalent to $\ker M(\mathbf{p},c)=\{0\}$,
hence to positive definiteness of $\GVM=M(\mathbf{p},c)^*M(\mathbf{p},c)$.
Therefore
\[
  \QM(f)=\ip{\GVM\mathbf a}{\mathbf a}
  \ge \lambda_{\min}(\GVM)\norm{\mathbf a}_2^2
  = \AM^2\norm{f}_{L^2}^2.
\]
The upper bound follows by summing Lemma~\ref{lem:upper} over the $N$ sample points.
\end{proof}

\section{Layer~B: Comparison Theorem}\label{sec:layerB}

All results here are conditional on~(T.2).

\begin{lemma}[Spectral stability via Weyl]\label{lem:C}
Assume~(T.2) with parameter $\varepsilon_N$.
Then
\[
  \lambda_{\min}(\GVM)
  \ge \lambda_{N-1}(c)-\norm{\EN}_2
  \ge \lambda_{N-1}(c)-\varepsilon_N
  >0,
\]
by Weyl's inequality \cite[Thm.~4.3.1]{HornJohnson2013}.
\end{lemma}

\begin{theorem}[Spectral decomposition with remainder]\label{thm:layerB}
Assume~(T.2) with $\varepsilon_N<\lambda_{N-1}(c)$.
For every $f=\sum_n a_n\psin\in V_{N,c}$,
\[
  \QM(f)=\ip{\Kop f}{f}_{L^2}+R_N(f),
  \qquad |R_N(f)|\le\varepsilon_N\norm{f}_{L^2}^2.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
In the PSWF basis,
\[
  \ip{\Kop f}{f}_{L^2}
  =\sum_n \lambda_n(c)|a_n|^2
  =\ip{G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}\mathbf a}{\mathbf a}.
\]
Hence
\[
  \QM(f)=\ip{\GVM\mathbf a}{\mathbf a}
  =\ip{G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}\mathbf a}{\mathbf a}+\ip{\EN\mathbf a}{\mathbf a}.
\]
Set $R_N(f):=\ip{\EN\mathbf a}{\mathbf a}$.
Then
\[
  |R_N(f)|\le \norm{\EN}_2\norm{\mathbf a}_2^2
  \le \varepsilon_N\norm{f}_{L^2}^2.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Structural bottleneck]
The key quantitative issue for any future application is to prove that
$\norm{\EN}_2$ is small relative to $\lambda_{N-1}(c)$ for structured
sampling sets.
Without such control, Layer~B remains a formal comparison principle.
For Gauss--PSWF nodes, Proposition~\ref{prop:gauss_error} and
Corollary~\ref{cor:gauss_weight_flatness} reduce this problem to quantitative
flatness of the quadrature weights.
\end{remark}

\section{Layer~C: Heuristics for prime sampling}\label{sec:layerC}

This section is explicitly heuristic.
The Weil explicit formula involves prime sums of the form
$\sum_p \log p\,|\widehat h(\log p)|^2$,
which motivates considering prime sampling sets.
To obtain coercivity from prime sampling one would need both
unisolvence and condition~(T.2), and both problems are open.

\begin{heuristic}[Prime sampling stability]
In the regime $N\le (2/\pi-\varepsilon)c$, one may hope that PSWF
concentration and prime-gap information combine to yield an estimate of the form
$\norm{\EN}_2=O(e^{-\delta' N})$.
This is currently speculative.
\end{heuristic}

\section{Open problems}\label{sec:open}

\begin{problem}[Uniform-grid unisolvence]
Determine whether uniform grids are unisolvent for $V_{N,c}$.
More generally, decide whether nontrivial zero-counting bounds hold for
linear combinations of the first $N$ PSWFs.
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Unisolvence for prime sampling]
Is prime sampling unisolvent for $V_{N,c}$?
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Condition~(T.2) for structured point sets]
Prove~(T.2) for prime sampling or derive quantitative weight-flatness estimates
for Gauss--PSWF nodes strong enough to trigger Corollary~\ref{cor:gauss_weight_flatness}.
Does a Marcinkiewicz--Zygmund inequality hold for PSWFs at prime nodes?
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Coupled limits]
Analyze $\lambda_{\min}(\GVM)$ and the competition between
$\norm{\EN}_2$ and $\lambda_{N-1}(c)$ in regimes where $N$ and $c$
vary simultaneously.
\end{problem}
