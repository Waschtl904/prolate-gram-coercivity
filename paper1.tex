\begin{abstract}
Let $\PW$ be the Paley--Wiener space of $\omega$-bandlimited functions
and $V_{N,c}:=\operatorname{span}\{\psi_0^{(c)},\dots,\psi_{N-1}^{(c)}\}$
the $N$-dimensional prolate subspace at time-bandwidth product $c=\omega T$.
For $N$ distinct sampling points $\mathbf{p}\subset[-T,T]$ we study
the discrete prolate Gram form $\QM(f)=\sum_{j=1}^N|f(p_j)|^2$ on $V_{N,c}$.

We prove unisolvence for random configurations and for weighted sampling models
satisfying a Marcinkiewicz--Zygmund-type lower frame bound.
We then show that the deviation between discrete sampling and the reference prolate spectral model
admits an exact structural decomposition into a weighted-frame defect and a spectral mismatch defect.
As a corollary, any regime in which both defects are simultaneously controlled yields
an explicit comparison formula
$\QM(f)=\ip{\Kop f}{f}_{L^2}+R_N(f)$ with $|R_N(f)|\le\varepsilon_N\norm{f}^2$.
We also record a PSWF-adapted quadrature model as a conjectural construction principle
for such defect control.
The paper is restricted throughout to fixed finite-dimensional regimes $(N,c)$.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Prolate spheroidal wave functions (PSWFs) provide a natural orthonormal basis
for finite-dimensional spectral truncations of the Paley--Wiener space associated
with the time--band limiting operator \cite{Slepian1978,BonamiKaroui}.
The resulting finite-dimensional spaces are classical objects in sampling theory,
reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces, and finite frame analysis.

This paper studies the stability of discrete sampling on the PSWF space
$V_{N,c}=\operatorname{span}\{\psi_0^{(c)},\dots,\psi_{N-1}^{(c)}\}$.
The linear-algebraic core is elementary: coercivity of the discrete sampling form
is equivalent to invertibility of the evaluation matrix $M(\mathbf{p},c)$.
The real content lies in two issues:
(i) identifying concrete families of sampling sets for which unisolvence can be proved,
and (ii) reducing the deviation between discrete sampling geometry and the reference spectral model
to explicit defect operators.
We therefore formulate the paper as an operator-theoretic and frame-theoretic decomposition result,
with spectral comparison appearing only after the relevant defects are controlled.
We restrict attention throughout to fixed finite-dimensional regimes $(N,c)$.

Section~\ref{sec:layerA} proves unisolvence for random sampling and for weighted sampling models
satisfying a lower frame inequality, and records the uniform-grid case as an open zero-counting problem.
Section~\ref{sec:layerB} proves the exact two-defect decomposition, introduces a quadrature-inspired candidate model,
and derives the spectral comparison theorem as a corollary under an ideal spectral-approximation regime.
The final section records several open problems and one heuristic construction principle.

\section{Setup and notation}\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Basic objects}

We use the engineering Fourier convention
$\widehat{f}(\xi)=\int_{\mathbb{R}}f(x)e^{-2\pi ix\xi}\,dx$.

\begin{remark}[Fourier convention and bandwidth]
Under this convention the Paley--Wiener space of bandwidth $\omega$
corresponds to Fourier support in
$[-\omega/(2\pi),\omega/(2\pi)]$.
Many references use the convention
$\widehat f(\xi)=\int f(x)e^{-i\xi x}\,dx$,
for which the same space corresponds to $[-\omega,\omega]$.
\end{remark}

The \emph{Paley--Wiener space} $\PW$ consists of all
$f\in L^2(\mathbb{R})$ with Fourier support in
$[-\omega/(2\pi),\omega/(2\pi)]$.
It is a reproducing-kernel Hilbert space with kernel
$K_{\PW}(x,y)=\tfrac{\omega}{\pi}\operatorname{sinc}(\omega(x-y)/\pi)$
and $K_{\PW}(x,x)=\omega/\pi$ \cite[Eq.~(3)]{BonamiKaroui}.
Every $f\in\PW$ extends to an entire function of exponential type,
by the Paley--Wiener theorem.

The \emph{prolate concentration operator} is
\[
  \Kop f(x)=\int_{-T}^{T}\frac{\sin\omega(x-y)}{\pi(x-y)}f(y)\,dy,
  \qquad \Kop:\PW\to\PW.
\]
It is self-adjoint and compact on $\PW$, with simple eigenvalues
$1>\lambda_0(c)>\lambda_1(c)>\cdots>0$ and eigenfunctions
(PSWFs) $\{\psin\}_{n\ge 0}$ forming an orthonormal basis of $\PW$
\cite{Slepian1978,BonamiKaroui}.
Throughout, $c=\omega T$ and
\[
  V_{N,c}:=\operatorname{span}\{\psi_0^{(c)},\dots,\psi_{N-1}^{(c)}\}.
\]
This is an $N$-dimensional subspace of $\PW$ for each fixed pair $(N,c)$.
There is no canonical identification of $V_{N,c}$ across different values
of $c$.
All operator comparisons in this paper are ultimately reduced to this finite-dimensional PSWF subspace.

\subsection{Gram matrix and reference matrix}

\begin{definition}[Prolate Gram matrix and form]\label{def:GramForm}
For distinct $\mathbf{p}=(p_1,\dots,p_N)\subset[-T,T]$, the
\emph{evaluation matrix} is $M(\mathbf{p},c)_{jn}:=\psi_n^{(c)}(p_j)$,
and the \emph{unweighted empirical Gram matrix} is
$\GVM:=M(\mathbf{p},c)^*M(\mathbf{p},c)$.

If $f=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}a_n\psin\in V_{N,c}$, then
$\norm{f}_{L^2}^2=\norm{\mathbf{a}}_2^2$ and
$\QM(f):=\sum_{j=1}^N|f(p_j)|^2=\ip{\GVM\mathbf{a}}{\mathbf{a}}$.
Write $\AM:=\sqrt{\lambda_{\min}(\GVM)}$ and
$\BM:=\sqrt{\lambda_{\max}(\GVM)}$.
Unweighted sampling corresponds to the special case $w_j\equiv 1$, which in general need not satisfy
frame bounds that are uniform with respect to the geometry of $\mathbf p$.
This unweighted quadratic form is the canonical Parseval-frame form of the evaluation system
associated with the chosen orthonormal basis.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Basis dependence and spectral invariance]\label{rem:basis_invariance}
The matrix $\GVM$ depends on the chosen orthonormal basis of $V_{N,c}$.
In this paper we use the PSWF basis, for which the coefficient identification is canonical.
However, the associated Gram operator on $V_{N,c}$ and its spectral quantities,
such as $\lambda_{\min}(\GVM)$ and $\lambda_{\max}(\GVM)$, are basis-invariant.
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}[Reference matrix]\label{def:ref_matrix}
Define the diagonal matrix
\[
  G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}:=\operatorname{diag}(\lambda_0(c),\dots,\lambda_{N-1}(c)).
\]
This is the matrix of $\Kop|_{V_{N,c}}$ in the PSWF eigenbasis:
for $f=\sum_n a_n\psin$,
\[
  \ip{\Kop f}{f}_{L^2}
  =\sum_n\lambda_n(c)|a_n|^2
  =\ip{G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}\mathbf{a}}{\mathbf{a}}.
\]
Set $\EN:=\GVM-G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Ideal spectral-approximation regime]\label{def:T2}
We say that $\mathbf{p}$ satisfies the ideal spectral-approximation regime if
\begin{enumerate}[(T.2a)]
\item the approximation condition
\[
  \norm{\EN}_2\le\varepsilon_N,
\]
\item and the spectral stability condition
\[
  \varepsilon_N<\lambda_{N-1}(c).
\]
\end{enumerate}
This is a strong spectral matching hypothesis and should not be regarded as generic.
Typically one should expect such a regime, if at all, only for specially constructed sampling or weighted-frame systems.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Geometric meaning of $\EN$]\label{rem:EN_meaning}
Thus $\EN$ measures the deviation between discrete sampling geometry and the reference spectral geometry of $\Kop$.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Bridge to constructive sampling questions]\label{rem:bridge_sampling}
The regime $\norm{\EN}_2\ll 1$ should be interpreted as a target condition, not as an a priori generic feature of arbitrary point sets.
One would expect it, if at all, from structured quadrature-like constructions, weighted Marcinkiewicz--Zygmund systems,
or other sampling geometries specifically designed to approximate the PSWF spectral projector on the finite-dimensional space $V_{N,c}$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{A conjectural construction principle}

The abstract condition~\textup{(T.2)} is deliberately nonconstructive and should be interpreted as a spectral matching principle rather than a generic property of point sets.
We now describe a class of sampling configurations that may serve as a concrete candidate mechanism for defect control.

\begin{definition}[PSWF-adapted quadrature model]\label{def:pswf_quad}
A weighted sampling system $(\mathbf p,D)$, with $D=\operatorname{diag}(w_1,\dots,w_N)$,
is called \emph{PSWF-adapted of order $N$} if it satisfies the approximate orthogonality relations
\[
\left|
\sum_{j=1}^N w_j\,\psi_m^{(c)}(p_j)\overline{\psi_n^{(c)}(p_j)}
- \lambda_n(c)\delta_{mn}
\right|
\le \varepsilon_N,
\qquad 0\le m,n\le N-1.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Quadrature interpretation]\label{rem:quad_interp}
Definition~\ref{def:pswf_quad} can be interpreted as a finite-dimensional quadrature rule
for the spectral projector of the concentration operator $\Kop$ restricted to $V_{N,c}$.
Equivalently, it asks for approximate discrete PSWF orthogonality with respect to the weighted sampling rule.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Relation to the ideal regime]\label{rem:quad_to_T2}
If $(\mathbf p,D)$ is PSWF-adapted in the sense of Definition~\ref{def:pswf_quad},
then the weighted defect matrix satisfies
\[
\norm{G^{\mathrm{w}}_{N,c}(\mathbf p,D)-G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}}_2 \lesssim \varepsilon_N,
\]
so the approximation part of the ideal spectral-approximation regime follows formally once the weighted-frame defect is also controlled.
The spectral stability condition is then a separation requirement between the PSWF spectral scale and the residual quadrature defect.
\end{remark}

\begin{heuristic}[Candidate constructions]\label{heur:quad_candidates}
Known constructions of high-order quadrature rules for oscillatory kernels,
including PSWF-based Gaussian quadrature and related methods \cite{Xiao2001},
suggest that PSWF-adapted systems should exist at least in regimes where $N\lesssim c$.
However, no general existence theorem guaranteeing the ideal spectral-approximation regime is currently known.
\end{heuristic}

\begin{remark}[Coefficient-space identification]\label{rem:coeff_ident}
The matrices $\GVM$ and $G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}$ are both viewed as operators on
$\mathbb C^N$ through the coefficient map
$f=\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}a_n\psi_n^{(c)}\longleftrightarrow \mathbf a=(a_0,\dots,a_{N-1})$.
Under this identification, $\GVM$ encodes the unweighted discrete sampling quadratic form
and $G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}$ encodes the restriction of $\Kop$ to $V_{N,c}$.
This identification is unitary only on the finite-dimensional space $V_{N,c}$ equipped with the PSWF basis,
not on the whole Paley--Wiener space.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Invariance of $V_{N,c}$ under $\Kop$]\label{rem:K_invariant}
Because the PSWFs are eigenfunctions of $\Kop$ on the whole space $\PW$,
the finite-dimensional subspace $V_{N,c}$ is invariant under $\Kop$.
The matrix $G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}$ is therefore exactly the matrix of the restricted operator
$\Kop|_{V_{N,c}}$ in the PSWF basis.
\end{remark}

\section{Unisolvence and operator core}\label{sec:layerA}

\begin{definition}[Unisolvent set]\label{def:unisolvent}
A set $\mathbf{p}=(p_1,\dots,p_N)\subset[-T,T]$ of $N$ distinct points
is \emph{unisolvent for $V_{N,c}$} if the only $f\in V_{N,c}$ satisfying
$f(p_j)=0$ for all $j$ is $f\equiv0$.
Since $\dim V_{N,c}=N$, this is equivalent to bijectivity of the evaluation map
$\mathcal{E}_{\mathbf{p}}:V_{N,c}\to\mathbb{C}^N$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Weighted frame model on $V_{N,c}$]\label{def:mz_model}
A family of nodes and positive weights
\[
  \{(p_j,w_j)\}_{j=1}^N, \qquad -T\le p_1<\cdots<p_N\le T, \qquad w_j>0,
\]
is said to satisfy a \emph{Marcinkiewicz--Zygmund frame condition} on $V_{N,c}$
if there exist constants $0<A\le B<\infty$ such that
\[
  A\norm{f}_{L^2(-T,T)}^2
  \le \sum_{j=1}^N w_j |f(p_j)|^2
  \le B\norm{f}_{L^2(-T,T)}^2
\]
for all $f\in V_{N,c}$.
The special case $A=1-\delta$, $B=1+\delta$ with $\delta\in[0,1)$ recovers
an approximately isometric model.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Weighted-frame viewpoint]\label{rem:weighted_frame}
Definition~\ref{def:mz_model} treats discrete sampling on $V_{N,c}$ as a weighted frame problem.
The geometry is therefore encoded not by an exact quadrature oracle but by frame bounds $(A,B)$.
Gauss--PSWF constructions should be regarded as motivation for such models, not as an automatic source of exact identities.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Why unisolvence is non-trivial for $V_{N,c}$]
For a Chebyshev system, every $N$-tuple of distinct points is unisolvent
\cite[Ch.~1]{KarlinStudden1966}.
The space $V_{N,c}$ is not known to be a Chebyshev system in general.
Hence unisolvence must be proved separately for specific families.
In particular, the natural zero-counting route for uniform grids remains open.
\end{remark}

\begin{lemma}[Identity principle for Paley--Wiener functions]\label{lem:identity}
If $f\in\PW$ vanishes on a set with a limit point in $\mathbb R$, then $f\equiv 0$
on $\mathbb R$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Every $f\in\PW$ extends to an entire function of exponential type.
If it vanishes on a set with a limit point in $\mathbb R$, then the entire extension has a non-discrete zero set.
By the identity theorem for holomorphic functions, the entire extension
must vanish identically, hence $f\equiv 0$ on $\mathbb R$.
Classically, this is a basic uniqueness phenomenon for Paley--Wiener spaces.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Weighted MZ lower frame implies unisolvence]\label{prop:mz_unisolvent}
Assume that $\{(p_j,w_j)\}_{j=1}^N$ satisfies the lower frame bound
\[
  A\norm{f}_{L^2(-T,T)}^2\le \sum_{j=1}^N w_j |f(p_j)|^2
\]
for all $f\in V_{N,c}$, with some constant $A>0$.
Then $\mathbf p=(p_1,\dots,p_N)$ is unisolvent for $V_{N,c}$.
In particular, every Marcinkiewicz--Zygmund frame condition with $A>0$
implies unisolvence.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If $f\in V_{N,c}$ satisfies $f(p_j)=0$ for all $j$, then
\[
  0=\sum_{j=1}^N w_j|f(p_j)|^2\ge A\norm{f}_{L^2(-T,T)}^2.
\]
Hence $f=0$ almost everywhere on $[-T,T]$, thus everywhere on that interval
by continuity, and therefore $f\equiv0$ on $\mathbb R$
by Lemma~\ref{lem:identity}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Random sampling is almost surely unisolvent]
\label{prop:random}
Let $p_1,\dots,p_N$ be i.i.d. with a distribution absolutely continuous
with respect to Lebesgue measure on $[-T,T]$.
Then $\mathbf{p}$ is unisolvent for $V_{N,c}$ almost surely.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Non-unisolvence means $\det M(\mathbf{p},c)=0$.
Since each PSWF $\psi_n^{(c)}$ is a real-analytic function, the map
$(p_1,\dots,p_N)\mapsto\det M(\mathbf{p},c)$ is real analytic.
It therefore suffices to know that this determinant is not identically zero.
Because the functions $\psi_0^{(c)},\dots,\psi_{N-1}^{(c)}$ are linearly independent,
and because point-evaluation functionals are separating on $V_{N,c}$,
a standard finite-dimensional argument yields a choice of $N$ points for which the evaluation matrix is invertible.
Equivalently, the determinant is analytically non-degenerate and hence not the zero function.
Its zero set therefore has Lebesgue measure zero, and absolute continuity implies
that non-unisolvence occurs with probability zero.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Pointwise bound via reproducing kernel]\label{lem:upper}
For every $f\in\PW$ and every $x\in\mathbb R$,
\[
  |f(x)|^2
  =|\ip{f}{K_x}_{L^2}|^2
  \le \norm{f}_{L^2}^2\,\norm{K_x}_{L^2}^2
  = K_{\PW}(x,x)\,\norm{f}_{L^2}^2
  = \frac{\omega}{\pi}\,\norm{f}_{L^2}^2.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{theorem}[Operator core]\label{thm:layerA}
Let $(N,c)$ be fixed and let $\mathbf{p}$ be unisolvent for $V_{N,c}$.
Then:
\begin{enumerate}[(I)]
\item $\QM(f)\ge \AM^2\norm{f}_{L^2}^2$ for all $f\in V_{N,c}$.
\item $\QM(f)\le N(\omega/\pi)\norm{f}_{L^2}^2$ for all $f\in V_{N,c}$.
\end{enumerate}
In particular, these bounds hold for every weighted frame model satisfying the hypotheses of
Proposition~\ref{prop:mz_unisolvent}, and by Proposition~\ref{prop:random} for random sampling almost surely.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Unisolvence is equivalent to $\ker M(\mathbf{p},c)=\{0\}$,
hence to positive definiteness of $\GVM=M(\mathbf{p},c)^*M(\mathbf{p},c)$.
Therefore
\[
  \QM(f)=\ip{\GVM\mathbf a}{\mathbf a}
  \ge \lambda_{\min}(\GVM)\norm{\mathbf a}_2^2
  = \AM^2\norm{f}_{L^2}^2.
\]
The upper bound follows by summing Lemma~\ref{lem:upper} over the $N$ sample points.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Rayleigh-quotient form of the upper bound]\label{lem:lambda_max_bound}
For every set of distinct points $\mathbf p\subset[-T,T]$,
\[
  \lambda_{\max}(\GVM)=\sup_{\norm{\mathbf a}_2=1}\ip{\GVM\mathbf a}{\mathbf a}
  =\sup_{\substack{f\in V_{N,c}\\ \norm{f}_{L^2}=1}} \QM(f).
\]
Consequently, if $\QM(f)\le B\norm{f}_{L^2}^2$ for all $f\in V_{N,c}$,
then $\lambda_{\max}(\GVM)\le B$.
In particular,
\[
  \lambda_{\max}(\GVM)\le N\omega/\pi.
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Using the PSWF isometry between $\mathbb C^N$ and $V_{N,c}$,
one has $\norm{f}_{L^2}=\norm{\mathbf a}_2$ and
$\QM(f)=\ip{\GVM\mathbf a}{\mathbf a}$.
The first identity is therefore just the Rayleigh-quotient characterization of the largest eigenvalue
of the Hermitian matrix $\GVM$.
The implication with constant $B$ is immediate.
Applying Theorem~\ref{thm:layerA}(II) gives the final bound.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Crudeness of the global upper bound]
The bound $\lambda_{\max}(\GVM)\le N\omega/\pi$ is intentionally crude, dimension-only,
independent of $c$, non-asymptotic, and not sharp in the PSWF regime.
Its role is to provide a geometry-free estimate suitable for the fixed-$(N,c)$ framework of this paper.
Sharper bounds exploiting prolate concentration are left for later work.
\end{remark}

\section{Structural reduction and comparison}\label{sec:layerB}

All results here take place after restriction to the finite-dimensional PSWF space $V_{N,c}$.

\begin{definition}[Weighted discrete reference matrix]\label{def:weighted_ref}
Given positive weights $w_1,\dots,w_N$, let $D:=\operatorname{diag}(w_1,\dots,w_N)$ and define
\[
  G^{\mathrm{w}}_{N,c}(\mathbf p,D):=M(\mathbf p,c)^* D\,M(\mathbf p,c).
\]
This is the weighted discrete Gram matrix associated with the sampling model $(\mathbf p,D)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Two Gram objects]\label{rem:two_grams}
The matrices $\GVM$ and $G^{\mathrm{w}}_{N,c}(\mathbf p,D)$ are built from the same evaluation matrix
$M(\mathbf p,c)$ and therefore from the same PSWF samples.
They differ only in whether one inserts the weight matrix $D$.
Thus the decomposition in Theorem~\ref{thm:main_reduction} separates two analytically distinct
contributions, but not two statistically or geometrically independent sources of randomness.
The splitting is algebraic, but not probabilistically or geometrically decoupled.
This coupling obstruction prevents a naive decoupling of geometric and spectral sampling errors.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Structural reduction theorem]\label{thm:main_reduction}
Let $(\mathbf p,D)$ be a weighted sampling model with positive diagonal weight matrix $D$.
Then
\[
  \EN
  =\GVM-G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}
  =M(\mathbf p,c)^*(I-D)M(\mathbf p,c)
   +\bigl(G^{\mathrm{w}}_{N,c}(\mathbf p,D)-G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}\bigr).
\]
Consequently,
\[
  \norm{\EN}_2
  \le \norm{I-D}_2\,\norm{M(\mathbf p,c)}_2^2
   + \norm{G^{\mathrm{w}}_{N,c}(\mathbf p,D)-G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}}_2.
\]
Thus the entire deviation between discrete sampling and the PSWF reference model is reducible
to a weighted-frame defect and a spectral mismatch defect.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Insert and subtract the weighted discrete Gram matrix:
\[
  \EN
  =\GVM-G^{\mathrm{w}}_{N,c}(\mathbf p,D)
   +G^{\mathrm{w}}_{N,c}(\mathbf p,D)-G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}.
\]
Since
\[
  \GVM-G^{\mathrm{w}}_{N,c}(\mathbf p,D)
  =M(\mathbf p,c)^*(I-D)M(\mathbf p,c),
\]
the claimed identity follows. The norm bound is immediate from subadditivity and submultiplicativity.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Interpretation of the reduction theorem]
The error splits into a weighted-frame defect
$M^*(I-D)M$ and a spectral mismatch term
$G^{\mathrm{w}}_{N,c}(\mathbf p,D)-G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}$.
These two terms are analytically distinct, but structurally coupled through the same evaluation matrix.
Any constructive route to spectral comparison must therefore control both mechanisms simultaneously.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Ideal-regime criterion from two defects]\label{cor:two_defects}
Assume that the weighted sampling model $(\mathbf p,D)$ satisfies
\[
  \norm{I-D}_2\le \delta_N,
  \qquad
  \norm{G^{\mathrm{w}}_{N,c}(\mathbf p,D)-G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}}_2\le \eta_N,
\]
with
\[
  \delta_N\,\frac{N\omega}{\pi}+\eta_N<\lambda_{N-1}(c).
\]
Then the ideal spectral-approximation regime from Definition~\ref{def:T2} holds with
\[
  \varepsilon_N:=\delta_N\,\frac{N\omega}{\pi}+\eta_N.
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
By Theorem~\ref{thm:main_reduction},
\[
  \norm{\EN}_2
  \le \norm{I-D}_2\,\norm{M(\mathbf p,c)}_2^2
   +\norm{G^{\mathrm{w}}_{N,c}(\mathbf p,D)-G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}}_2.
\]
Using Lemma~\ref{lem:lambda_max_bound},
$\norm{M(\mathbf p,c)}_2^2=\lambda_{\max}(\GVM)\le N\omega/\pi$.
Therefore
\[
  \norm{\EN}_2\le \delta_N\,\frac{N\omega}{\pi}+\eta_N=\varepsilon_N.
\]
The displayed hypothesis gives $\varepsilon_N<\lambda_{N-1}(c)$, so Definition~\ref{def:T2} applies.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Spectral stability via Weyl]\label{lem:weyl_stability}
Assume the ideal spectral-approximation regime with parameter $\varepsilon_N$.
Then
\[
  \lambda_{\min}(\GVM)
  \ge \lambda_{\min}(G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c})-\norm{\EN}_2
  = \lambda_{N-1}(c)-\norm{\EN}_2
  \ge \lambda_{N-1}(c)-\varepsilon_N
  >0,
\]
where the first inequality is the standard Hermitian perturbation bound
$\lambda_{\min}(A+E)\ge \lambda_{\min}(A)-\norm{E}_2$ applied on $\mathbb C^N$
under the coefficient-space identification of Remark~\ref{rem:coeff_ident}.
\end{lemma}

\begin{remark}[Eigenvalue versus eigenvector stability]\label{rem:weyl_scope}
Lemma~\ref{lem:weyl_stability} only gives eigenvalue stability.
It does not by itself provide control of eigenvector alignment between the discrete Gram matrix and the reference spectral model.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Spectral comparison in the ideal regime]\label{cor:spectral_comparison}
Assume the ideal spectral-approximation regime with $\varepsilon_N<\lambda_{N-1}(c)$.
For every $f=\sum_n a_n\psin\in V_{N,c}$,
\[
  \QM(f)=\ip{\Kop f}{f}_{L^2}+R_N(f),
  \qquad |R_N(f)|\le\varepsilon_N\norm{f}_{L^2}^2.
\]
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
By Remark~\ref{rem:coeff_ident}, the coefficient identification is unitary on $V_{N,c}$ in the PSWF basis.
In the PSWF basis,
\[
  \ip{\Kop f}{f}_{L^2}
  =\sum_n \lambda_n(c)|a_n|^2
  =\ip{G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}\mathbf a}{\mathbf a}.
\]
Consequently,
\[
  \QM(f)=\ip{\GVM\mathbf a}{\mathbf a}
  =\ip{G^{\mathrm{ref}}_{N,c}\mathbf a}{\mathbf a}+\ip{\EN\mathbf a}{\mathbf a}.
\]
Set $R_N(f):=\ip{\EN\mathbf a}{\mathbf a}$.
Then
\[
  |R_N(f)|\le \norm{\EN}_2\norm{\mathbf a}_2^2
  \le \varepsilon_N\norm{f}_{L^2}^2.
\]
This corollary isolates the spectral comparison as a consequence of the reduction theorem plus defect control.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Structural bottleneck]
The key quantitative issue for any future application is to prove that
$\norm{\EN}_2$ is small relative to $\lambda_{N-1}(c)$ for structured
sampling sets.
By Theorem~\ref{thm:main_reduction}, this requires simultaneous control of a weighted-frame defect
and a spectral mismatch defect.
Without such control, the reduction theorem remains structural rather than constructive.
\end{remark}

\section{Open problems and heuristic directions}\label{sec:layerC}

The results of this paper isolate the finite-dimensional operator structure of discrete PSWF sampling.
The remaining questions concern constructive sampling models, sharper estimates, and asymptotic regimes.

\begin{problem}[Uniform-grid unisolvence]
Determine whether uniform grids are unisolvent for $V_{N,c}$.
A natural intermediate step would be to establish oscillation bounds or near-Chebyshev properties
for linear combinations of the first $N$ PSWFs on $[-T,T]$.
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Weighted frame models for PSWF sampling]
Construct explicit structured sampling sets and weights satisfying a Marcinkiewicz--Zygmund frame condition on $V_{N,c}$.
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Ideal spectral approximation from two defects]
Derive quantitative estimates on the weighted-frame defect and the spectral mismatch defect
strong enough to trigger Corollary~\ref{cor:two_defects}.
\end{problem}

\begin{problem}[Coupled limits]
Analyze $\lambda_{\min}(\GVM)$ and the competition between
$\norm{\EN}_2$ and $\lambda_{N-1}(c)$ in regimes where $N$ and $c$
vary simultaneously.
\end{problem}

\begin{heuristic}[Quadrature-based construction principle]
PSWF-adapted quadrature systems should be viewed as a natural candidate mechanism
for realizing the ideal spectral-approximation regime in a constructive way.
This remains open at the level of general existence theorems.
\end{heuristic}
